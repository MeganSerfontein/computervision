< 2018 Final Exhibition >
Untitled 1 | Various Versions
Java program, personal computer, monitor, webcam
This installation makes use of a Java program I wrote, to intervene in how the device shows what the camera is recording. It requires you to stand still for a long time in order to generate a clear image, plotting 80 000 pixels per frame out of a 307 200 pixel canvas. This allows for ghost imaging, and unique artworks that are controlled and drawn by interaction. In this manner, it is the viewer who becomes the subject and the artwork – whether they realise it at first or not – and their image will remain for a little while after they leave. This is done to not only query the treatment of digital identity as gimmick and game, but also the underlying unease present in recording yourself with or without consent, and the imprint of yourself therein. For this work, and my other pieces, I rely on installation as the form of display, as I find the devices as important as the program, or perhaps even more so. Having the technological objects on display as a pose to hidden draws on themes of familiarity or alienation with the object depending on who you are, and therefore effects how you choose to interact with the piece. This is as important as how you choose to interact with the digital mediation of yourself and your inhabited space that the machine translates back to you.
Untitled 2 | Various Versions
Java program, personal computer, monitor, webcam
This piece uses a program that analyses what the camera sees, and compares each frame to what was recorded before it. It compares the pixels of the current frame to the one before it, and generates an image based on similarity and difference. If the pixels are too "different" (Red vs Blue), it will paint that pixel black. Those that are similar remain white. In this way, the piece only draws what it deems to be adequate movement, and requires interaction in order to show anything other than a white screen. As with Untitled 1, the manner of display being installation - with all the wires and devices out in the open - is important to the work. It is indicative of the interconnected processes that are being run in order to take your image and show it back to you. And in the case of my programming pieces, it shows how this network can be interrupted, and altered.

< smartphones, selfies, and surveillance >

Untitled 3 | Installed at GUS Gallery Stellenbosch 2018 | Artist taking the photograph
5 smartphones, chargers
This work consisted of 5 smartphones (each with their own working cameras) that would watch the space in front of their lenses. Some of them were recording, and some were not. Others were used by the audience to take selfies. These smartphones, when in relation to the programming pieces, functioned as a way to access them visually, and become aware of the fact that the space was watched and recorded by the devices. Smartphones are the most commonly used communication devices, and I found them to be the largest conversation starter around the idea of networking, interconnectivity, and even privacy invasion, which speaks well into the theme of surveillance.

< MAPPING DATA IN SPACE AND TIME >

Light
Dark
These programs are my newest additions, and focus on picking out one piece of data to map. In the videos below, "Light" was a test with my smartphone flashlight to see how difinitive and different a variable (the value of the pixel in this case) should be in order to get clear "motion detection". This method can be used for any colour, as well as for "magnitude of motion" when combined with my prior black and white piece, where it instead will focus on largest motion detected. The video "Dark" is a more graphical drawing of a room where the program was taught to find the most black pixels, and then draw lines outward in any random radius where the length depended on how close to (0,0,0) the pixel was.

< ABSTRACTING DATA, MOTION DETECTION, PLAY >

The process of creating my body of work was an exercise of learning, play, and learning to consolidate my work and play. To come to a place where drawing was made through gesture, and mediated by a machine, was a frustrating but ultimately delightful experience. Having always used programming and technology as a tool for so long, it was strange to break that mould of web design, and look at it through a Fine Art lens.

< ARTIST STATEMENT >

Having grown up surrounded by technology in the form of games and communities, I developed an early interest in how technology can influence and curate a person's sense of self. This digital fascination sparked an engagement with the concept of a technologically-driven perception of reality, specifically where the self, and self within space - be it digital or physical - is concerned. My work uses simple methods of intervention, inspired by modern phenomena such as the selfie, vlogs (video blogs), surveillance, and digital effects, and is brought about through the practice of programming and interactivity. My work is also heavily dependent on the technological objects themselves. The level of interactivity with the works is influenced by the pre-existing relationships between people and objects such as a phone, screen, or camera. My work investigates how technology has influenced the way we interact and see. It focuses on the mapping of spaces both digital and physical, and the influence of a digitised world on our physical one. Additionally, it looks at how the way we choose to inhabit and interact with that space is influenced and shaped by technology.
